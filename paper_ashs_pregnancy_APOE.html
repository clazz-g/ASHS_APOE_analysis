2 Introduction

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a neurodegenerative disorder characterised by the accumulation of pathophysiological abnormalities leading to neurodegeneration and progressive cognitive and functional decline. Ageing, carring the apolipoprotein E E4 (APOE4) and having female chromosomal sex are three established non-modifiable risk factors for the typical late-onset of this disease [1]. There is growing evidence showing an interaction between APOE4 and biological sex: whilst men carrying the APOE4 allele see their risk increase by …, women’s risk increase by …. Amongst APOE4 carriers, women exhibit an increased risk of conversion from healthy ageing to MCI, or from MCI to AD dementia between the ages of 65 to 75 [2,3]. Factors underlying the interaction between APOE4 and sex are not well understood, warrating a deeper investigation of sex specific events which might interact with this phenomenon. In particular, pregnancy is a female-specific event with far reaching biological impacts that are not yet well-explored.

In humans, pregnancy is associated with drastic increases in sex-steroid levels [source] and a significant hippocampal volume loss [4], which is detectable for up to at least 6 years post-partum in primiparous women [5]. Interestingly, the dynamic volume changes seen during gestation are associated with the chnage curve seen in in free circulating sex-hormones (check source, carmona). In rodents, these changes are linked with decreased hippocampal neurogenesis during gestation and the postpartum period [8,9]. Crucially, the hippocampus is one of the first regions affected by AD, and whole hippocampal volume is a hallmark predictor of conversion from MCI to AD [6]. Therefore, this region seems of particular interest given it is affected by parity and is related to AD neurodegeneration.

Evidence on how previous pregnancies influence AD risk is conflicting. Amongst epidemiological studies that explored a potential association between parity and AD, some report a link between multiparity and greater cognitive decline [7], whilst other report no link or the opposite findings [8,9.,10] Additionally, a post-mortem histopathological study of 42 women, with clinical diagnosis spanning the spectrum from normal aging to definite AD, found that multiparity was associated with a greater number of neuritic plaques [11]. However, analysis of Aβ-PET data from 248 women, including those with CU or clinically diagnosed MCI, found no association between Aβ burden and parity or multiparity [19].

These inconsistent findings may be understood through the lens of Brinton’s “healthy cell bias” theory, which posits that estrogen’s neuroprotective effects are contingent upon neuronal metabolic health. Under physiological conditions, estrogen enhances glucose metabolism and mitochondrial bioenergetics, which supports the energy-intensive process of Ca²⁺ homeostasis—mitochondria require substantial ATP to sequester excess cytoplasmic Ca²⁺ and maintain proper signaling dynamics. However, when metabolic injury is already present and Ca²⁺ regulation has been disrupted, estrogen exposure can paradoxically exacerbate neurodegeneration rather than prevent it. ApoE4 creates precisely this vulnerable metabolic state by impairing glucose metabolism and lysosomal function, essential for Ca²⁺ homeostasis [ https://doi.org/10.1038/s41420-025-02600-y]. Consequently, ApoE4 carriers may exist in a state of chronic metabolic compromise potentially rendering them susceptible to the significant surge in estrogen which occurs during the gestation period.

3 Methods

3.1 Study population

We included postmenopausal women who completed the baseline and first longitudinal visit (with an average time between visits of 3.32 years) from the ongoing ALFA+ study (Clinicaltrials.gov, NCT01835717). ALFA+ is a research cohort of middle-aged cognitively unimpaired participants, many of whom are offspring of AD patients, who have been deeply characterized by clinical interviews, lifestyle and risk factor questionnaires, cognitive testing, CSF biomarkers, and neuroimaging procedures, including magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and Aβ positron emission tomography (PET). All of these procedures are repeated every 3 years with the main aim of identifying the earliest pathophysiological changes in the preclinical AD continuum [14]. Briefly, ALFA+ inclusion criteria were: 1) participants who had previously participated in the 45-65/FPM2012 study (ALFA parent cohort); 2) age between 45 and 75 years at the moment of inclusion in the cohort; 3) long-term commitment to undergo all tests and study procedures (MRI, PET, and lumbar puncture). Exclusion criteria at baseline included: 1) cognitive impairment (Clinical Dementia Rating [CDR] > 0, Mini-Mental State Examination [MMSE] < 26, semantic fluency < 12); 2) any significant systemic illness or unstable medical condition which could lead to difficulty complying with the protocol; 3) any contraindication to any test or procedure; 4) family history of monogenic AD. In addition, women with a history of cardiovascular-related gestational disease, such as preeclampsia, were excluded from our sample for analysis. Sex and menopausal status, defined as absence of menstruations for 12 consecutive months, were self disclosed. Women who reported being premenopausal or perimenopausal were not included in our analysis either.

The study was approved by the Independent Ethics Committee “Parc de Salut Mar”, Barcelona, and all participants gave written informed consent. Baseline visits took place between 2016 and 2019, and the follow-up visit between 2019 and 2022.

The sample consisted of 254 participants at baseline visit, and 211 at follow-up (Table 1). Out of the 43 participants who dropped out of the study between visits, 30 withdrew for personal reasons, 3 became unresponsive, 2 died, 5 showed incidental l findings on their structural MRI, and 3 were excluded due to mild cognitive impairment clinical diagnosis.

3.2 Reproductive variables

Parity was defined as the number of biological children and used as a continuous and dichotomous [parous vs nulliparous] variable in the main analyses. Due to the small number of grand multiparous participants, anyone with 4 or more biological children was grouped together. Hormonal Replacement Therapy (HRT) use was defined as a binary variable [currently using vs not currently using]. Cause of menopause was also defined as a binary variable [natural vs non-natural]. Reproductive span was calculated by subtracting age at menarche from age at menopause. Early menopause was defined as a binary variable [age at menopause> 45 vs age at menopause≤ 45]. These informations were self-reported by participants during an interview with a clinician at the baseline visit. When asking about age at menopause, participants were asked to report their age 6(?) months after their last period.

3.3 Cognitive measures

Cognition was defined using 5 composite scores that measured sparate congitive domains categorised as follows: attention, executive functions, memory, language and visual processing. Z-scores were computed for Visit 1 and 2 and used in the linear regressions.

3.4 AD biomarkers

CSF collection, processing, and storage in the ALFA+ study have been described previously [15]. Briefly, CSF Aβ42 and Aβ40 were measured with the exploratory Roche NeuroToolKit immunoassays (Roche Diagnostics International Ltd, Rotkreuz, Switzerland) on a cobas e 601 module. Measurements were performed at the Clinical Neurochemistry Laboratory, Sahlgrenska University Hospital, Molndal, Sweden. Aß accumulation (Aß+, Aß–) was estimated using CSF Aß42/40. Aß status (Aß+, Aß–) was defined using the cutoff of 0.071 for the ratio Aß42/40 [23], where any participant below this cutoff was considered to be Aβ+.

3.5 Imaging data acquisition and preprocessing

Automatic Segmentation of Hippocampal Subfields (ASHS) software [16] was used on the T1, T2 and inversion recovery (IR) weighted images to segment the hippocampal formation in the following sub-regions: Brodmann areas 35 and 36, cornu Ammonis (CA) 1, 2 and 3, dentate gyrus (DG), entorhinal cortex (ERC), parahippocampal cortex (PHC), subiculum (SUB), and the hippocampal sulcus (SUL). All segmentations were visually inspected before proceeding with the statistical analyses.

Hippocampal values are reported in cm3 and were obtained at both baseline and follow-up visits.

Gray matter volume was obtained using Freesurfer7.

Total Intracranial volume (TIV) was estimated using deformation fields obtained during the nonlinear registration of each subject’s T1-weighted MRI to a whole-brain template. The FSL Brain Extraction Tool [17] was applied to the template to create an intracranial mask, which was warped into each subject’s MRI at native space to obtain the TIV measurement.

3.6 Statistical analyses

We used linear mixed-effects models to assess the joint effects of parity and APOE-e4 (exposures), on HV and cognition z-score (outcomes) over both visits, and test if this association is dependent on time. These models included a three-way interaction term among parity, APOE4, and time (the interval between Visit 1 and Visit 2 in years) and all lower-order interactions. Cognitive analyses were adjusted for years of education, and age at baseline visit. HV analyses were additionally adjusted for TIV.

Specificity of the results was tested by testing the 3-way interaction between parity, APOE4 and time on gray matter volume changes in the calcarine cortex.

To test for potential confounders, analyses that showed a significant parity x APOE-e4 interaction were reproduced adding Aβ load as a covariate to disentangle the neuropathological contributions of Aβ accumulation from the phenogenetic contributions of APOE4. Given the high correlation between Aβ load and APOE4 variables, collinearity was tested for each model and can be found in the suppplementary materials. In addition to this, we reproduced the analysis in Aβ-negative participants only. After that, the following reproductive variables were also added as covariates to test if hormonal variables partly explained the effect at hand: HRT, early menopause, reproductive span and cause of menopause.

Additional exploratory mixed-effects linear regressions were performed to explore the interactives effects of parity APOE-e4 and time on hippocampal subfield volumes across visits. Subfields values were standardized for comparison purposes. Given the exploratory nature of these analyses, results are reported without correction for multiple comparisons. Lastly, we conducted similar linear regression using inflammatory biomarkers as outcomes to investigate their role in the observed association. These analyses were normalised using box-cox transformation to ensure that the distribution of residuals remained normal.

Statistical significance was set at p < 0.05. Analyses were conducted using R software, version 4.5.1 (The R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria). The package ‘emmeans’ was used to calculate estimated marginal mean for direct group comparison of predicted effects [18]. The package tidiverse was used for data processing and plotting [19]. The package gtsummary was used for formatting tables [20]. The package ggeffects was used to extract predicted values from regression models [21]. The package lme4 was used for performing mixed effect linear regression models [22]. The package MICE was used for multiple imputation of missing data.

Rates of missing data in exposures or outcome variables at each time point are reported (Table 1).

4.2 Interplay between parity, APOE-E4 and HV decline

Parity, APOE-ε4, and time showed a significant three-way interaction for total HV (β = -0.019, SE = 0.006, p = 0.001), indicating that higher parity was associated with steeper decline in HV among APOE-ε4 carriers (Figure X).

Stratifying by APOE-ε4 status yielded a significant interaction between parity and time in carriers only (β = -0.012, SE = 0.004, p = 0.001) (Figure X).

Reproducing the analysis using parity as a dichotomous variable reduced the 3-way interaction estimate and significance of the effect (β = -0.031, SE = 0.016, p = 0.051).

The 3-way interaction between APOE4 parity and time was not associated with GMV in the calcarine cortical area.

4.3 Adjusting for potential confounding factors

To control for other potential confounding factors, the main analysis was first rerun including Aβ load as a covariate. The triple interaction effect was maintained when adjusting analyses by Aβ accumulation (β = -0.026, SE = 0.008, p = 0.001). However, Aβ load showed a notable association with the outcome (β = 1.754, SE = 0.944, p = 0.064), indicating it may play a role independently. When reproducing the analysis including only participants with negative Aβ status, the effect was maintained, although attenuated (CSF Aβ<0.071 pg/ml, n = 128) (β = -0.03, SE = 0.012, p = 0.016).

Interestingly, covarying the models stratified by APOE4 status for AB did not modify the effect of parity and time on HV in carriers (β = -0.014, SE = 0.005, p = 0.003), but it yielded a signficant interaction of parity and time on HV in non-carriers (β = 0.013, SE = 0.006, p = 0.033), which was not the case without adjusting for it (Figure X).

Additionally, replicating the analysis by further adjusting for hormonal variables (HRT, reproductive span, early menopause, cause of menopause) still yielded a significant 3-way estimate (β = -0.02, SE = 0.006, p = 0.002), suggesting these variables could not fully explain the association.

4.4 Cognitive trajectory

The interaction between parity, APOE4 and time yielded a weak association with attention score, which was not present after FDR correction. None of the other scores showed any longitudinal association with our main predictors or 3-way interaction.

4.5 Exploratory analysis of hippocampal subfields

The 3-way interaction between parity, APOE4 and time was significant in the CA1 (β= -0.036, SE = 0.013, p= 0.006), CA2 (β= -0.068, SE = 0.029, p= 0.023) and in the DG (β= -0.033, SE = 0.015, p= 0.029). Similarly, parity was associated with steeper HV decline in APOE-E4 carriers.

After stratifying the analysis by APOE4 status, only the carrier group remained significant in the CA1 (β= -0.024, SE = 0.008, p= 0.003), CA2 (β= -0.043, SE = 0.018, p= 0.02) and DG (β= -0.023, SE = 0.01, p= 0.025).

Adjusting whole group analysis by AB led to a stronger effect in the CA1 (β= -0.054, SE = 0.016, p= 0.001), but attenuated significance in the CA2 (β= -0.057, SE = 0.038, p= 0.132). Effects in the DG remained largely unchanged (β= -0.044, SE = 0.019, p= 0.025).

4.6 Exploratory analysis of inflammatory and cardiovascular biomarkers

Parity showed a weak association with YKL-40 in APOE4 carriers nearing significance, but this association was independent of time (β= -0.005, SE = 0.003, p= 0.082).

5 Discussion

Our study aimed to investigate the impact of parity and APOE4 allele on hippocampal volume and cgnitive change in CU post-menopausal women at risk of AD. Our results suggest that parity and AD-related genotype interact in women to impact hippocampal volume loss. Specifically, APOE4 carriers showed an association between parity and steeped HV loss, but the opposite was true in non carriers. Stratified analyses revealed that the interactive effects of parity and time were only significant in APOE4 carriers. These interactive effects were not explained by potential confounding factors such as AB accumulation or other reproductive variables. Exploratory analyses of hippocampal subfields showed a significant association between the 3-way interaction and the CA1 and DG after adjusting for AB load. No association with cognition was found. Together, these findings highlight that being a carrier of the APOE4 allele may…

Association between higher parity and steeper HV reduction in APOE4 carriers

The main finding of our study was that APOE4 carriers showed an association between higher parity and steeper hippocampal decline. Our results evoke that previous parity may confer a deleterious effect on HV, a hallmark marker of early AD-related neurodegeneration, in carriers on the APOE4 allele. Although APOE4 carriers showed significantly higher accumulations of AB load, adjusting for this variable did not seem to explain the observed effect. Moreover, this effect was also seen after reproducing the same analysis with AB negative subjects only. Therefore, our results do not seem to reflect amyloid driven neurodegeneration, which can affect the hippocampaus in early stages of the disease (Raffa), but instead an independent pathway specific to APOE4 and parity. These results coincide with our previous hypothesis that the physiological and metabolical stress induced by pregnancy might be mitigated by initial presence of disease risk. Carrying the APOE4 allele is associated with increased inflammation in young adults (Ringman 2012), and this altered inflammatory response might induce greater stress in response to the high neuroplastic demands that occur during pregnancy.

Similarly to whole hippocampal analyses, stratified analyses in APOE4 subgroups showed that the effect was driven by carriers of the allele. Interestingly, Lee and colleagues found that primiparity increased neurogenesis in wildtype rats, but reduced it in APOE4 rats. Changes in these areas could be linked with parity related shanges, given the CA1 is among the impacted areas of the hippocampus during pregnancy in humans (Pritschet 2023) and rodents (Puri richard Galea 2023), and parity increases DG volumes in rodents.

Our findings mirroir the ones found by Lee and colleagues in rodents. Similarly, female APOE4 rodent models showed an association between parity and lower neurogenesis in the ventral hippocampus. In addition to this, the DG was one of the impacted regions in our sample.

Effect of parity increases with each additional child

Unlike in Lee’s study, our sample included multiparous women. Replication of the main analysis usign parity as a dichotomous variable reduced the size of the estimate and its significance. This suggests that parity is a better predictor when used as a continuous variable. Therefore, the interactive effect of parity with APOE4 and time seem to increase with every additional biological child. However, our sample lacked in grand multiparous women, which has been associated with higher AD/cogntive decline. There might be a non linear association between parity and out outcome which might be missed by the limitation of our data.

Possible mechanisms behind the effect

Our data did not find a significant link between inflammation-related CSF biomarkers and the interactive effects of APOE4, parity and time. Inflammatory processes serve essential physiological roles in parutrition (Challis 2009). Long-term consequences remain understudied and several reproductive adjacent variables seem to also impact this long-term association like sex of the child, age at first pregnancy or time between pregnancies (Zavatta 2021). Interestingly, neuroimmune and inflamatory processes see a lesser increase with age in parous rats, compared with nulliparous rats (durate-guzman 2019). In Lee et al., primiparous middleaged hAPOE4 rats showed a greater inflammatory phenotype compared to wildtype rats in the ventral hippocampus, suggesting that APOE4 might also modify parity-related long-term inflammatory responses. However, the ability to directly test presence of these markers in the regions of interest may partly explain the lack of significant relationship in our sample

No effect on cognitive changes

The effects seen on the hippocampus did not trasnlate to an effect on any of the cognitive domains examined. A previous study done with the UK biobank also finds no effect of the interaction between APOE4 and parity on cognitive score (lindseth et al 2023), although their battery of test is limited. In our case, this lack of association might not indicate absence of associaiton but rather reflect the relatively short amount of time between visits, and limited number of follow-ups. Since neurodegeneration typically precedes cognitive symptoms, our window of observation might be capturing early changes which could, in turn, translate into cognitive changes.

The AFLFA+ cohort is an ideal cohort to test this interaction, given the participants are enriched in genetic AD risk, As such, about 40% of our cohort is a carrier of the APOE4 allele, meaning group interactions are very balanced statistically.

Some limitations should be considered. Firstly, the number of follow-up visits is insufficient to observe nonlinear evolution, therefore limiting our understanding of possible mechanisms underlying AD pathogenesis. Future work should include additional time points to better understand the trajectory of these results. Secondly, although our sample’s excellent health record makes it ideal to identify subtle changes in the early AD continuum, the vast majority of ALFA+ study participants are white Caucasians, resulting in an ethnically homogeneous sample primarily from middle to upper-middle class backgrounds. Future work should reproduce these analyses in a more diverse cohort to understand the impact of ethnicity and social background on the present results. Lastly, the absence of direct hormonal measurements limits our ability to draw definitive conclusions about the role of sex steroid hormones in the observed associations. By relying on proxy measures, we can only estimate hormone-related effects, potentially leading to an underestimation of their actual influence.

7.1 MRI data acquisition

MRI scans were acquired on a in-house 3.0 T scanner (GE Discovery MR750 W 3 T) using the same protocol for all participants, which included one T1- and three T2- weighted sequences. The 3D-T1w sequence was designed with an isotropic voxel size of1mm3 and a matrix size of256x256x160 (TR/TE/TI = 8.0/3.7/450 ms, NSA = 1, flip angle = 8°). Three 3D-T2w sequences, with a voxel size of1 mm× 1 mm× 3 mm, were also used: fluid attenuation inversion recovery (FLAIR: TR/TE/ TI = 11,000/90/2600 ms, flip angle = 160°), fast spin echo (FSE: TR/TE = 5000/85 ms, flip angle = 110°) and, gradient echo (GRE: TR/TE = 1300/23 ms, flip angle = 15°). All scans were visually assessed to verify their quality and to detect incidental findings by a trained neuroradiologist and have been reported elsewhere (Brugulat-Serrat et al. 2017). At visit 1, ten participants were excluded due to the presence of a meningioma, as well as 37 participants due to susceptibility, motion artefacts or segmentation problems, resulting in a total of 561 images available for subsequent analysis.
